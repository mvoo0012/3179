Skin Cancer in Australia (Asignment 2 FIT3179)

Skin cancer is one of the most common types of cancer in Australia, largely due to the country's high levels of ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

This dashboard presents key insights into the distribution of skin cancer across Australia, including:


Mortality rates across different states

Risk of skin cancer according to age groups

Diagnosis and survival rates based on cancer stages


By exploring these aspects, the dashboard aims to provide a comprehensive overview of skin cancer's impact in Australia,

highlighting crucial patterns and trends that can help in understanding and managing this widespread disease.

Graph 1: Heatmap of skin cancer distribution in Australia (2007-2020)

The heat map shows skin cancer incidents and death cases across Australia (normalised with per 100,000 population),

with a time slider from 2007 to 2020 and menu selection for genders, and types of case.


Queensland and New South Wales consistenly, show the highest skin cancer and death cases,

which are explainable with their tropical climate and higher UV exposure levels


Victoria, South and Western Australia also have significant death and general cases,

which might be due to their large and considerable UV exposure


Tasmania also exhibit significant cases per 100,000 population at times, which could be

due to its low population but moderately high UV exposure or skin cancer cases

Graph 2: Bubble chart of Skin Cancer Incidence vs. Mortality by State (2007-2020)

The bubble plot illustrates the relationship between skin cancer incidence (number of cases) and mortality (deaths) across Australia states, from 2007-2020. Each point represents a state or territory,

with the size correspond to its population. The graph is also mapped using a log scale, highlighting that a large number of cases might have relatively lower death rates,

suggesting the effectiveness of early diagnosis and treatment. The time slider and menu slection for gender and skin cancer type provides a more detailed and thorough look at the data/visualisation


New South Wales and Queensland show high skin cancer incidence as well as elevated mortality, possibly indicating a significant public health challenge.

Smaller territories like Australian Capital Territory and Northern Territory have relatively lower incidence and mortality, likely due to their smaller populations and possibly differing UV exposure levels.

Graph 4: Radar Chart for Cancer Risk (Diagnosis and Death) In Australia (2007-2024)

The radar chart represents skin cancer risk of being diagnoised and dying across different age groups in Australia from 2007 to 2024.

Each axis on the chart corresponds to a specific age group, showing how the risk of developing and dying from skin cancer changes as people age. It features functionality

of time slider, gender and cancer type menu selections,to aid the exploring expereince of how age, gender and cancer type affect skin cancer risk in Australia

The orange region represents the risk of death by cancer, whereas the blue area reflect the risk of being diagnosed

through different age ranges


The largest portion of the chart indicates that individuals from age 40 and above face a greater risk of developing

and dying from melanoma. The risk continues to grow with age, peaking in older age groups

(especially before age 70 and 80), likely due to accumulated exposure to UV radiation over time.


The chart shows that the risk of melanoma is relatively low for individuals under age 30, though the potential for

future risk exists, emphasizing the importance of early preventive measures such as sun protection

Graph 5: Stacked Bars Chart of Skin Cancer Diagnosis and Survival by Stage and Age Group, 2011

The stacked bar graphs show the distribution of skin cancer diagnosis(left) and survival(right) acriss various stages of cancer (I to IV),

segmented by different age groups. There is also a menu selection for gender so data can be filtered in more details


Stage I has the highest number of diagnoses and survivors, with the majority of cases occurring in the 60–69 and 70-79 age group.

This suggests that middle-aged and older adults are most at risk of developing skin cancer, but

they also benefit from earlier detection and treatment.


Stage IV represents the least number of diagnoses, indicating that only a small number of cases progress to the most severe stage.

Survival rates also drop significantly in Stages III and IV, iemphasizing the critical need for early diagnosis

and intervention. Later-stage skin cancer presents more challenges for treatment and successful outcomes.


Missing Data Considerations: It is important to note that there is a significant amount of missing data for survival cases in
